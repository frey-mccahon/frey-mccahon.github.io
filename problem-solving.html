Problem Solving



My experience with the suggested problem solving echniques


Pseudocode:
I can see the utility in writing pseudocode, but in this week's exercises I didn't find myself using it much because I wasn’t too sure of the steps on how to solve the problems, and found myself experimenting a lot which didn’t lend it’s self to starting with pseudocode.

Trying something:
This is what I mainly did, I was constantly trying things, and most of the time not getting the result I was hoping for.

Rubber Ducky Method:
This method I found very useful. Talking through each step of my code to myself was very essential. At this point in my learning journey I am very easily confused and lost. Walking through the chain of events in my code and what each step does was essential in ensuring I was understanding what was going on.

Reading Error Messages:
This was an essential and unavoidable part of the process. Reading errors and running code through the console log are your eyes and ears in the dark caves of the code lands. Though my confidence with reading error messages admittedly needs work, I’m often intimidated by a big block of red unfamiliar and technical text.

Googling:
Lots of googling was happening during this week’s exercises. Maybe even TOO much! If I found I wasn’t quickly gelling with the language or descriptive approach of the page, I would just go back to the google results and click on another result.

Asking Peers for Help:
This is also something I was doing very often. Most of these problems I was doing in concert with other students I message with, and when other of us made a break through we would bring our findings to each other.

Asking Coaches for Help:
This was probably the most useful of all the problem solving methods. It was often my last resort, but provided the most fruitful results. Often my questions or problems I had with a task wasn’t one isolated thing, but rather a cluster of conceptual questions all loosely linked, so google wasn’t much help.

Improving my Process with Reflection:
This is definitely something that could use improvement. When I’m finished with an exercise, I’m usually so relieved that I push it away and move on. This is a bad habit and doesn’t lend it’s self to practical learning. I should reflect more on the tasks I have completed to better reenforce what I’ve learned / areas I need to direct more focus on.
